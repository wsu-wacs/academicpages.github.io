Web and Complex Systems Lab

Dept. of Computer Science and Engineering
Kno.e.sis Research Center
Wright State University


We study, develop, and then apply novel network science, deep learning, statistical graph, and nonparametric methods to model and analyze complex systems. We tackle a variety of machine learning and data science research problems and emphasize method, model, and systems development. Our focus is to develop techniques that are practically transferrable or enhance some complex system analysis. Recent examples include the integration of Bayesian models and recurrent neural networks for web server cache prefetching, probing convolutional neural network activations to classify semantically meaningful input concepts, devising new machine learning algorithms over dynamic networks, adaptating notions of statistical consistency to cluster trajectory data, improving hyper-parameter searches and using GANs for data augmentation to build simplicial complices of data, measuring social dynamics in online emotional support systems, and in combining deep embedding models with knowledge bases to measure the semantic-similarity of emoji. We have applied our methods to better measure online social systems, to evaluate emoji usage, to model wide-area geospatial dynamics, to understand the impact of web robot traffic on web systems, and to build practical methods that explain the actions of deep learning systems.

Lab members are adept at writing open software and publishing open data.

Prospective Students

We are not recruiting new PhD students. We may have open MS positions and probably have open undergraduate research positions.

We are only considering MS students with a math or statistics background at this time. The ideal candidate is a student with Math/Stat/CS/CSE major and Math/Stat/CS/CSE minor. You have ideally taken a machine learning course (online or undergrad) and have implemented some ML algorithms by hand in the past. Inexperience with ML can be replaced with a strong math and stats background. Students must also be an excellent written and oral communicator. Research productivity is expected.

WSU Undergrads who have a high GPA, finished MTH 2310, is confident in programming and using computers, actually liked CS 2200, and has an interest in AI / machine learning / data science can reach out to explore independent study and (if available) undergrad research positions.

Latest News (see the archive for past updates)


1/20/18:Lakshika successfully defends her thesis on studying gang member profiles on Twitter. She will be joining Gracenote as a research engineer in Okland, CA this summer. **Congratulations Lakshika!**


1/16/18: Some (non-)Universal Features of Web Robot Traffic accepted at IEEE Conference on Information Sciences and Systems. This study identifies statistical properties of web robot traffic that are common and contrasting across multiple web servers around the world. Great job Mahdieh!


1/14/18: Contrasting Web Robot and Human Behaviors with Network Models accepted for publication in the Journal of Communications. The article contrasts web robot and human visititation behaviors through the lens of network analytics. Congratulations Kyle!


11/15/17: Relating Input Concepts to Convolutional Neural Network Decisions accepted at NIPS Workshop on Explainable Machine Learning. This work develops a novel algorithm to find distributed representations of input concepts in a convolutional neural network. It then studies how types of representations could affect the decisions the deep learning system makes. Well done Ning!


11/3/17: An article on EmojiNet has been picked up by Psychology Today. Neat!


11/3/17: EmojiNet, our machine readable sense inventory for Emoji co-developed by Lakshika, has been selected for publication as a Kaggle dataset. Check it out here. If you play around with it let us know what you find!


10/10/17: Realistic Traffic Generation for Web Robots accepted at IEEE Intl. Conference on Machine Learning and Applications 2017. This work presents a generative process for producing synthetic sequences of web robot traffic. A stochastic process moderates temporal properties while a Bayesian model assigns behavioral attributes. The synthetic traffic impacts the performance of web server caches in similar ways as real robot traffic. Well done Kyle!
